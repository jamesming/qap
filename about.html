The Queer Asylum Project is dedicated to a world where people are free to self-determine and self-express without fear, and we believe that will require greater awareness of and sensitivity to the suffering of others.

We believe there are important stories to be told, and that peoples' voices need to be heard. It is hoped that publishing these personal narratives—moving, and in some cases epic in their proportions—will educate the informed and the uninformed, alike.


Vision
The Queer Asylum Project is committed to a world where every human being is free to self-determine, self-identify and self-express their sexuality without fear.


Mission
The Queer Asylum Project provides a platform for lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgendered/ing individuals to share their stories of persecution so that others might be more informed of and sensitized to such injustices.


Who
QAP is maintained by Richard Forhez and David Wishart.

QAP was envisioned in 2002 by Richard Forhez. Later, local philanthropist Kevin Grady generously provided the angle funding to get started. Then-business partner Brian Reed and a handful of colleagues and friends developed the project over time.


Why
It is well-documented that people all over the world are commonly mistreated because of their real or perceived sexual orientation. People of all ages suffer every form of persecution, ranging from verbal taunting, to torture and murder. What’s more, these transgressions are often sanctioned and/or even perpetrated by parents or other relatives, teachers, religious institutions, and officials at every level of government, the military, and law enforcement.

This became real for the founders of QAP when a friend—an immigration attorney who specializes in asylum cases—shared a story about a young man who suffered relentless mistreatment by schoolmates, teachers, the police, and even his own father—several times to the point of hospitalization. Buchi (his nickname) finally took matters into his own hands in order to have a better life—perhaps even saving it. The story, it seemed, was screaming to be told. And so QAP was born. Or at least conceived.

The Queer Asylum Project provides a platform for lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender individuals to share their stories of persecution so that people everywhere can be informed of and sensitized to these injustices.
